John McCarthy (computer scientist)

1927-2011

"Father Of Artificial Intelligence (AI)"

John McCarthy was an American computer scientist and one of the founding figures of artificial intelligence (AI). Born on September 4, 1927, in Boston, Massachusetts, he made groundbreaking contributions to AI, programming languages, and computer science. He coined the term "Artificial Intelligence" in 1956 and developed the LISP programming language, which became fundamental for AI research. McCarthy also pioneered the concept of time-sharing in computing, allowing multiple users to share computing resources efficiently. Throughout his career, he worked at MIT, Stanford, and Dartmouth, shaping AI's theoretical and practical foundations. His "Advice Taker" concept laid the groundwork for automated reasoning systems. For his contributions, he received several prestigious awards, including the Turing Award (1971) and the National Medal of Science (1990). His legacy continues to influence AI research and development today. McCarthy passed away on October 24, 2011, leaving behind a lasting impact on the field of artificial intelligence.
